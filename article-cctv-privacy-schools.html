CCTV in Schools: What the Law Requires and What Good Practice Looks Like

CCTV is standard practice in UK schools. Most headteachers, business managers and governors accept that cameras are a reasonable and necessary part of running a safe site. What is less well understood is the legal framework that governs how those cameras must be deployed, what footage can be used for, and what obligations a school takes on the moment it becomes a data controller for surveillance footage. Getting this wrong is not just an administrative oversight. It can expose the school to ICO enforcement action, undermine trust with parents and staff, and potentially render footage inadmissible in a safeguarding investigation.

The Legal Framework

The moment a school operates a CCTV system, it is processing personal data. That brings it squarely under UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018. The Information Commissioner's Office publishes detailed guidance on CCTV in schools, and its Code of Practice is the standard against which any system will be measured if a complaint or enforcement action arises.

The key principles every school needs to understand are straightforward in concept, even if the detail requires care.

Lawful basis. The school must identify a lawful basis for processing surveillance footage. In most school contexts, this will be legitimate interests, covering safeguarding, crime prevention and protection of property. The basis must be documented.

Purpose limitation. Footage can only be used for the purposes it was collected for. If a camera is installed for perimeter security, using the footage to monitor staff performance is a separate and unjustified purpose that would need its own lawful basis.

Data minimisation. Only collect what you actually need. This principle directly informs camera placement. If a camera in a particular location is capturing footage that is not necessary for the stated purpose, its placement is hard to justify.

Transparency. Individuals must know they are being filmed. This means clear, readable signage at all entrances and throughout the site, and a CCTV policy that is accessible to staff, parents and pupils. It does not mean obtaining consent from every person on site, but it does mean not operating cameras covertly.

Retention limits. Footage should only be kept for as long as necessary. The ICO recommends that most schools should retain footage for no more than 30 days unless there is a specific reason to keep it longer, such as a live investigation. Footage that is kept indefinitely is a compliance risk.

Security. Footage is personal data and must be stored securely. Access should be restricted to authorised personnel only, with a clear process for who can request access and on what grounds.

Where Cameras Can and Cannot Go

This is the area where schools most commonly get into difficulty, usually not through deliberate misuse but through unclear thinking at the point of installation.

Appropriate locations include the main entrance and reception area, the school perimeter and all gates, car parks and drop-off areas, external walkways and corridors, communal spaces such as the playground, sports hall and canteen, and storage areas containing high-value equipment. The common thread is that these are spaces where people have a lower reasonable expectation of privacy and where the surveillance purpose is clear.

Prohibited locations are absolute. Toilets, changing rooms and showers cannot be monitored under any circumstances. There are no exceptions. Schools sometimes ask about changing rooms in relation to theft, but the answer is still no. The right approach is better physical security for storage, not surveillance.

Staff rooms and private offices are generally not appropriate for CCTV. Staff have a reasonable expectation of privacy in these spaces, and surveillance would require a specific, documented and compelling justification that would be very difficult to meet in practice.

Classrooms are the most frequently debated area. The ICO's position is that CCTV in classrooms is generally not appropriate. The threshold for justification is high, and it must be based on a specific, documented risk rather than a general desire to monitor behaviour or performance. If a school is considering classroom cameras, it should take legal advice first.

CCTV and Behaviour Management

Many schools use CCTV footage as part of investigations into incidents involving pupils. This is a legitimate use, provided the footage was collected for a purpose that includes safeguarding and investigation of serious incidents.

What CCTV should not become is a primary behaviour management tool. Using surveillance to monitor pupils' day-to-day behaviour, rather than to investigate specific serious incidents, risks creating an environment that undermines trust and has a chilling effect on normal behaviour. It is also likely to be disproportionate and therefore legally questionable.

The practical test is whether you would be comfortable explaining to a parent or governor exactly what the camera is there for and how the footage is being used. If the answer requires significant qualification, the use is probably not well-founded.

The CCTV Policy Your School Needs

A CCTV system without a written policy is a compliance problem waiting to happen. The policy does not need to be lengthy, but it does need to cover specific things.

It should state clearly why the school operates CCTV, including the legal basis for doing so. It should identify the Data Controller and the individual responsible for day-to-day compliance, typically the Data Protection Officer where one is appointed. It should set out exactly which cameras are in place and what areas they cover. It should specify how long footage is retained and how it is stored. It should describe who has authority to access footage and the process for doing so. It should include the procedure for handling Subject Access Requests, since any individual whose image has been captured has the right to request a copy of relevant footage under UK GDPR. And it should set out how complaints about the use of the system will be handled.

The policy should be published on the school website and included in the staff handbook. It should be reviewed at least annually and whenever the system changes.

Practical Steps for Schools Already Operating CCTV

If your school already has a CCTV system in place, there are a few questions worth asking right now.

Is your CCTV policy up to date and publicly available? If not, that is the first fix. Have your camera locations been reviewed against the data minimisation principle? Are any cameras capturing footage that is not necessary for the stated purpose? Is your footage retention period clearly defined and actually being applied, or is footage accumulating indefinitely? Do your keyholders and IT staff know the process for handling a Subject Access Request involving footage? Is footage access logged so you can demonstrate who has viewed what and when?

None of these are difficult to address, but they require someone to own the task. In most schools, that sits with the DPO or business manager. If it has not been reviewed recently, now is a good time.
